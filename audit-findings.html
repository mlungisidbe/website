Writing Audit Findings
That Actually Get Fixed.

Mlungisi Dube
Tech Risk · Frameworks · Controls
7 Principles

Most audit findings die on paper. They are written, reviewed, issued — and then quietly ignored. Not because the organisation doesn't care about risk, but because the finding never gave anyone a compelling reason to act. Writing findings that actually get fixed is both a craft and a discipline. Here is what leading practice looks like.

7 Principles
01
Clarity

Lead with impact, not process.

The first thing your reader asks — consciously or not — is: "So what?" Answer that question in your opening sentence. Don't start with what you tested. Start with what is at risk. A finding that opens with "We noted that the control was not operating effectively" is not a finding — it is an observation. A finding that opens with "The organisation is exposed to X because Y" gives a decision-maker a reason to read on. Quantify the impact where you can. Risk ratings are useful, but a concrete number — financial, operational, reputational — is far more persuasive.

02
Specificity

Write for the person who has to fix it.

Audit findings are often written for the audit committee or senior leadership. But the person who has to implement the recommendation is usually a manager three levels below them. If your recommendation says "Management should strengthen controls" — that person has no idea what to do on Monday morning. Be specific. Name the process. Name the system. Describe the action in terms that a capable person in that role can execute without needing to interpret your intent. The clearer your recommendation, the fewer excuses there are for delay.

03
Root Cause

Separate the condition from the cause.

A finding has four components: condition (what is), criteria (what should be), cause (why the gap exists), and consequence (what it means). Most auditors get the condition right and skip the cause. This is a mistake. Management will not fix a problem they don't understand. If the root cause is a lack of training, say so. If it is a poorly designed process, say so. If it is a resourcing constraint, say so. Recommendations that target the root cause get implemented. Recommendations that target the symptom get repeated in next year's audit.

04
Language

Use plain language. Always.

Audit jargon is the enemy of action. Phrases like "control deficiency," "inadequate segregation of duties," and "insufficient evidence of operating effectiveness" mean something to auditors. They mean very little to a CFO under pressure or an operations manager reading your report between meetings. Write at the level of a smart, informed non-specialist. If you need to use a technical term, explain it. Short sentences. Active voice. One idea per paragraph. The easier your finding is to read, the harder it is to dismiss.

05
Engagement

Agree the recommendation before you issue the report.

The biggest predictor of whether a finding gets fixed is whether management agreed to fix it before the report landed in their inbox. This is not about softening findings — it is about removing the surprise that triggers defensiveness. Share draft findings early. Walk the relevant owner through the observation and ask: "Does this reflect what you understand to be happening?" and "What would a realistic remediation look like?" A management response that is genuinely theirs — not a boilerplate commitment written under time pressure — is far more likely to be executed.

06
Accountability

Set deadlines that reflect reality.

A 30-day remediation deadline for a complex technology control change is not ambitious — it is fantasy. And when management misses it, everyone loses credibility: management for missing the date, audit for setting an unrealistic one. Work with the owner to set a target date that is challenging but achievable. Break large remediations into milestones. A finding with three 90-day milestones is easier to track and hold accountable than a single 12-month deadline that nobody revisits until it is overdue.

07
Follow-Through

Follow up. Relentlessly.

Writing a great finding is half the job. The other half is tracking whether anything actually changed. Build a follow-up rhythm into your audit cycle. Check in at the milestone dates. When a deadline slips, escalate proportionately — not as punishment, but because the risk is still open. The findings that get fixed are the ones where management knows someone is watching. The ones that get forgotten are the ones where the audit team issued the report and moved on to the next engagement.

Closing Thought
The audit finding is the product of weeks of work. Don't let it fail in the last mile because it was too vague, too jargon-heavy, or too disconnected from the person who has to act on it. Write findings that give people a reason to care — and a clear path to fix the problem.
