Writing Audit Findings
That Actually Get Fixed.

Mlungisi Dube
Tech Risk · Frameworks · Controls
7 Principles

Most audit findings die on paper. They are written, reviewed, issued, and then quietly ignored. Not because the organisation doesn't care about risk, but because the finding never gave anyone a compelling reason to act. Writing findings that actually get fixed is both an art and a discipline. Here is what I have learned on what works

7 Principles
01
Clarity

Lead with impact, not process.

The first thing your reader asks, consciously or not is: "So what?" Answer that question in your opening sentence. Don't start with what you tested. Start with what is at risk. A finding that opens with "We noted that the control was not operating effectively" is accurate but leaves unanswered questions. A finding that opens with "The organisation is exposed to X because Y" gives a decision-maker a reason to be interested immediately. Why whould they care? Quantify the impact where you can. Risk ratings can be useful for this - A concrete number if available is even more persuasive.

02
Specificity

Write for the person who has to fix it.

Observations should be written with the audience in mind. One of these people is the person who has to implement the recommendation. Here I mean someone who has power to make the decision to implement, not the person pressing a button. If your recommendation says "Management should strengthen controls"; This gives no clarity for what needs to be done on Monday morning. Be as specific as you can be without designing the control. Name the process that is broken. The system that is broken. Where it is broken. The clearer your recommendation, the fewer excuses there are for delay.

03
Root Cause

Separate the condition from the cause.

There is a difference between what you test ( What should be), What you find ( What is), The cause (why the gap exists), and consequence (What it means/What could happen). The report should make clear each of these elements, even if they are not called out explicitly. Problems that are not understood will rarely be fixed. Understand the root cause of the failures you call out. Your assessment should be detailed enough to find these details. Is it a lack of training? If it because the process is poorly designed and would never mitigate the risk in its current state?. This element is also tied to what you would recommend to solve the problem. Recommendations that target the root cause get implemented. Recommendations that target the symptom get repeated in next year. Unless you enjoy having these type of conversations over and over - Strike at the root - not branches

04
Language

Use plain language. Always.

Jargon is the enemy of action. There can be no action without understanding. No actions when questions continue to linger. Phrases like "control deficiency," "inadequate segregation of duties," and "insufficient evidence of operating effectiveness" mean something to an experienced practitioner. They mean very little to a Head of Operations or a Finance Director reading your report between innumerable meetings. Write at the level of a smart, informed non-specialist. If you need to use a technical term, explain it. Abbreviations should also be clear/explained. Use short sentences. Active voice. One idea per paragraph. The easier your finding is to read, the harder it is to dismiss.

05
Engagement

Agree the factual accuracy of the observation before you issue the report.

This is not about softening findings.It is about removing surprises that trigger defensiveness. This is not a birthday party. Share draft observations early. Stick to the facts and don't embellish or extrapolate on endless possibilities. Walk the relevant owner through the observation and ask: "Does this reflect what you understand to be happening?" and "What would a realistic remediation look like?" . Get a response that is genuinely theirs, not one that is designed by you or shared under time pressure. That will gurantee delays or failure to remediate.

06
Accountability

Set deadlines that reflect reality.

A 30-day remediation deadline for a complex technology control change is not ambitious. It is J.R R Tolkien levels of fantasy. When that deadline is missed, everyone loses credibility. The target date should be challenging but achievable.

07
Follow-Through

Follow up.

Tracking whether anything actually changed is the other half tied to raising observations. A follow up rythm should be built into your testing/Audit cycle. One way to do this is to look at Issue closure dates and follow up on the remediation. When deadlines slip - there should be a mechanism to escalate that is proprortinal to the findings. Repeated deadline misses can quickly build a culture of unremediated findings where the risks remain open and the organisation remains exposed. The findings that get fixed are the ones which are monitored - Where there is accountabiility

Closing Thought
Observations/Findings are the product of weeks of work. Don't let it all fall on its face because it was too vague, too jargon-heavy, or too disconnected from the person who has to act on it or from reality. Write findings that give people a reason to care and a clear path to fix the problem.
