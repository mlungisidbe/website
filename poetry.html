Poetry

Words arranged with intention. Poems about identity, place, struggle, wonder — and the quiet spaces between certainty and doubt. Writing as a way of paying attention to what is otherwise too easily forgotten.

On Writing Poetry

Poetry is the most honest form of writing I know. Unlike prose, it cannot hide behind momentum. Every word has to earn its place. Every line break is a decision. The compression it demands forces a kind of clarity that other forms allow you to avoid.

The poems here move between the personal and the political, the intimate and the historical. Some are finished; others are working drafts that may change. All of them are attempts to say something true, in the fewest words possible.

Poems
Identity

What They Called Progress

On memory & erasure
I.
They came with maps already drawn, names already chosen, borders that cut through the bodies of people who had never heard the word for what was being done to them. We learned their language to survive and found, halfway through a sentence, we had forgotten the word for home.
A meditation on language loss and the colonial rewriting of identity. The poem asks what it means to carry a history that was deliberately obscured — and whether the act of naming it back is enough.
Technology

The Algorithm Decides

On automation & accountability
II.
No one signed it. No one chose the outcome. The model recommended, the system actioned, the human nodded without quite reading what it meant. Somewhere, a door closed. Somewhere, a number changed. Somewhere, a life bent around a decision no one made.
Written in response to thinking about algorithmic decision-making and the diffusion of responsibility in automated systems. When accountability is distributed across a pipeline, it can disappear entirely.
Place

Durban, 6am

On home & distance
III.
The Indian Ocean does not care about your credentials. It arrives the same way every morning — salt and certainty, the sound of something older than ambition. I left and the ocean stayed. That is the agreement every city makes with the people who leave it.
A poem about the specific texture of leaving a place you love — and the strange guilt of that leaving. Durban is where a great deal of who I am was formed, even from a distance.
